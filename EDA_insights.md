Global Economic Trends Analysis 
1. GDP Growth Distribution Analysis

Most GDP growth observations were concentrated between 0% and 6%, indicating relatively stable and moderate economic expansion across the majority of economies in the dataset.

The distribution showed a noticeable positive skew, meaning:

extreme high-growth observations occurred more frequently than extreme negative growth periods,
while most economies remained clustered around moderate growth levels.

The GDP growth boxplot revealed:

a median GDP growth rate of 2–3%,
several negative outliers below -10%,
and positive outliers reaching nearly 35%.
Negative GDP Growth Outliers

The lowest GDP growth values ranged between approximately -15% and -17%.

These extreme contractions may indicate:

recessions,
financial crises,
pandemics,
geopolitical instability,
or economic collapse.
Positive GDP Growth Outliers

The highest GDP growth observations ranged from approximately 20% to 35%.

Such unusually high growth may reflect:

rapid post-crisis recovery,
commodity booms,
infrastructure expansion,
export surges,
or industrial acceleration in developing economies.

Many top-growth economies exhibited Trade % values ranging from approximately 85% to 220%.

This suggests that:

economies strongly integrated into global trade networks may experience faster expansion,
especially resource-exporting economies.
High-growth economies often show low unemployment

Several high-growth observations corresponded with unemployment rates below 2%.

Examples included:

Kuwait (~0.8%)
Qatar (~0.45%)

This may indicate that:

Strong economic growth can stimulate labor markets,
increase industrial activity,
and generate employment opportunities.

0

No single variable strongly explains GDP growth independently,
Economic growth depends on multiple interacting factors.

These may include:

trade,
inflation,
investment,

industrialization,

GDP Growth and School Enrollment showed a negative relationship

The correlation between GDP growth and school enrollment appeared moderately negative.

Most countries with:

school enrollment between 80% and 120%

showed:

GDP growth is generally between 0% and 5%.

Possible explanation:

developed economies often maintain high educational attainment but slower, stable economic growth.
Trade and Unemployment showed a negative relationship

Countries with Trade % values above approximately:

200%

generally maintained unemployment rates below:

10%

Meanwhile, the highest unemployment observations (30–40%) occurred mostly among economies with trade percentages between:

50% and 120%

This suggests:

trade openness alone does not guarantee employment stability,
But highly export-oriented economies may support stronger labor markets.
